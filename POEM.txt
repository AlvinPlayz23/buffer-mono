The Last Call

He staggered home with whiskey breath,
The night a blur of cheer and mirth,
Each step a joke, each laugh a toast,
Unknowing that he’d lost the most.

He tipped his glass, he tipped his hat,
Said, "One more round!"—then felt quite flat.
The room went cold, the lights went dim,
A silence fell, no barroom hymn.

He blinked, and saw his own pale face,
A specter in a deserted place.
The bottles sang no more, no cheer—
For Death had come, and he was here.

Now sober in the afterlife’s stark gleam,
He laughs at life’s absurd dream:
“To think I drank to drown my fears,
When all along I’d died in years.”
